üìú General Rules (Page 1)

Introduction Rules

  • Fact in Question: If the prompt contains a fact, you must paraphrase it in the introduction.
  • Viewpoint in Question: If the prompt starts with a viewpoint, start your intro with a Fact or History first, then paraphrase the viewpoint.
  • Structure: General Fact / History + Paraphrased Viewpoint + Thesis Statement

Tense & Grammar

‚ö†Ô∏è Trend Questions: If the question describes a changing situation (trend), use the Present Continuous tense.
Example: "The number of people working from home is increasing."
‚ö†Ô∏è Comparison with Past: To compare today with the past, use the Present Perfect.
Example: "Technology has changed the way we communicate."
‚ö†Ô∏è General Statements: When making general statements, always use Plural Countable Nouns (not singular).
Correct: "Cars cause pollution." (Not: "Car causes pollution")

Cautious Language (Hedging)

Never say things with 100% certainty. Use hedging words to sound academic:

Use: would, often, could, is likely to, tends to, may, might.

üó£Ô∏è Opinion Essay Types

There are two main approaches: Strong View (100% agree/disagree) or Middle View (Balanced).

Note: You can take a Strong view for almost all questions. If you take a Middle view when a Strong view is possible, you might lose marks for clarity unless done perfectly.

Question Identifiers

  • Is this a positive or negative development?
  • To what extent do you agree or disagree?
  • What is your opinion?

üèóÔ∏è Introduction Structure (Strong)

Type 1: Simple

1. Paraphrase the Fact from the question.
2. Thesis: "I strongly believe that this is a positive/negative trend."
OR "I strongly believe that this is harmful/detrimental for..."

Type 2: With History (Better)

1. History/Fact: "While in the past [Past Situation], today [Current Situation]."
2. Stance: "I strongly agree/disagree with the idea that..."
3. Outline: Paraphrase the prompt requirement.
If no fact exists to paraphrase:
"It is often said that / It has been suggested that [Paraphrase Viewpoint]. I strongly agree/disagree with this point of view."

üìù Body Paragraphs (Strong View)

Go straight to the point. Support your side fully.

Body 1: Direct Reason 1 -> Explain (Why? So what?) -> Example -> Micro-conclusion (Overall...).
Body 2: Direct Reason 2 -> Explain -> Example.
Body 3 (Refutation): "Some may argue that [Opposing View]. However, [Refute/Reject it immediately]."
‚ö†Ô∏è "The Best" Trap: If the question uses absolutes like "The best" or "The only", you MUST take a Strong stance. Reject the idea that it is "the best" because:
- It is not applicable to all people.
- It is not effective in all situations.
- Other methods exist.

‚öñÔ∏è Middle View Strategy

Use this when the question has a "Too many / Too much" element (e.g., "Too many choices") or a double-barrelled statement (A is true because of B).

Introduction: Fact + "I think although [Less important side], [More important side]."
Body 1: "On the one hand..." (The side you agree with LESS).
Body 2: "On the other hand..." (The side you agree with MORE).
Conclusion: "In conclusion, I believe even though [Side A] is important because [Reason], [Side B] is essential since [Reason]."

*Note: A Middle view is not 50/50. It leans towards one side.

üí¨ Discussion Types

Type A: "Discuss both views and give your own opinion."
Type B: "Do the advantages outweigh the disadvantages?"

Type A: Discuss Both Views

There are 3 logic scenarios (The Apple/Orange Analogy):

Group 1: Apples are good.
Group 2: Apples are bad.
Group 1: Apples are good.
Group 2: Oranges are good.
Group 1: Apples are good.
Group 2: Apples are bad AND Oranges are good.

Structure (Strong Approach)

Introduction: "Some people (myself included / including me) argue that [Side A], whereas others believe that [Side B]."
Body 1 (Agreement): Discuss the side you agree with. Give 2 reasons.
Body 2 (Disagreement + Refutation): "Some people argue that [Side B]... However, [Refute it]."
Refutation Strategy:
- "This benefit can be achieved via other means."
- "This is not a priority right now."
- "This logic is flawed because..."

Structure (Middle Approach)

Body 1: 2 arguments for Side A.
Body 2: 2 arguments for Side B.
Conclusion: Summarize both and state your balanced view.

Type B: Advantages & Disadvantages

1. Yes/No Question ("Do the advantages outweigh...?")

You MUST take a position. Are there more Pros or Cons?

Strong Strategy:
Body 1 (Major Side): 2 Strong Advantages.
Body 2 (Minor Side + Refutation): "However, some minor disadvantages exist... [Minimize them]."
Conclusion: "Despite the minor drawbacks of X, it is my strong belief that the benefits are a net positive."

2. Wh- Question ("What are the advantages...?")

No opinion needed. Just list them.

Body 1: 2 Advantages.
Body 2: 2 Disadvantages.
Conclusion: Summarize all 4 points.

üåç Issues & Problems

This essay type has a Topic Statement (a problem/issue) but NO viewpoint given. You must analyze it.

Common Requirements

  • Causes: "Why is this the case?" / "What reasons are behind this?"
  • Consequences: "What problems does this cause?"
  • Solutions: "What can be done?"

Introduction Template

1. History/Context: "While in the past..., these days..."
2. Paraphrase Topic: Restate the problem.
3. Outline: "This essay will outline some possible reasons for this, as well as the social issues it may lead to / suggesting some solutions."

‚õìÔ∏è Analysis Mechanism (Body)

Do not use "Good/Bad" logic. Use "Cause/Effect/Solution" logic.

Linking Words for Causes

Because, The reason for this is that, Thanks to, Due to, Owing to.

Linking Words for Effects

As a result of, Therefore, Result in, Consequently, Lead to.

Language for Solutions

‚ö†Ô∏è Be Tentative: Solutions are potential, not guaranteed. Use "Could be", "Would be", "A possible solution".
"A possible solution to [Problem] could be..."
"One way to solve the problem of [X] would be..."
"This would alleviate/mitigate the issue by..."

Structure: Cause & Solution

Body 1 (Causes): Cause 1 -> Explanation -> Cause 2 -> Explanation.
Body 2 (Solutions): Solution 1 -> How it works -> Solution 2.
Conclusion: Summarize causes and solutions. Use "However" to transition from problem to solution.
"In conclusion, [Cause 1] and [Cause 2] have led to [Problem]. However, these problems can be dealt with by [Solution 1] and [Solution 2]."

üîÄ Mixed / Two-Part Questions

This combines an Opinion question with an Expository element (Causes/Effects/Solutions/Items).

‚ö†Ô∏è Golden Rule: The order of your Body Paragraphs MUST match the order of the questions in the prompt. Do not change the order.

Example Prompt

"How realistic is the expectation of job satisfaction? (Opinion) What factors contribute to it? (Items)"

Structure

Introduction: Answer the first question directly. Answer the second question vaguely (e.g., "There are several factors...").
Body 1: Answer Question 1 (Opinion).
Body 2: Answer Question 2 (Items/Factors).
Conclusion: Summarize both answers.

Developing "Items"

When asked for "Factors" or "Reasons" (Items), use this pattern:

First item: "The most significant factor is [X] because..."
Second item: "Another key element is [Y] since..."
Linking phrases: Besides, Apart from that, Furthermore (Do not use "Beside").

Special Case: "Effects"

If the question asks "How has technology affected relationships?", only discuss the change (Past vs Now). Do not say if it is good or bad yet (unless asked).

"Previously, people met in person. Now, they interact online." (This is a change).
"It is easier now." (This is an opinion/benefit - Avoid this in the 'Effects' paragraph).
